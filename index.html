About Celtic Biotech

Celtic Biotech is an Irish bio-pharmaceutical company developing novel peptides for the treatment of solid tumours, with a specific focus on non-small cell lung cancer. With current treatments often falling short in efficacy, safety, and patient convenience, the Company has identified promising naturally derived receptor binding proteins, one of which, CB-24 crotoxin, it has progressed into Phase I trials using a novel intra-patient dose escalating protocol.

The drug has shown excellent safety, minimal side-effects and stabilisation of disease in several patients in Phase Ib (6 patients evaluated) multi-cancer trials. Delivered as a user friendly at-home monotherapy using infusion pumps, the patients are monitored remotely requiring visits to clinic twice weekly for dose escalation. Part funded by the European Innovation Council, Phase Ic clinical trials will commence in 2024 to identify MTD and optimum treatment protocol, with a view to commencing Phase II trials in NSCLC 2025/26, in this potentially game-changing cancer therapeutic.

About Irish Cancer Society

The Irish Cancer Society is the leading national cancer charity in Ireland dedicated to transforming the experiences and outcomes of people affected by cancer, by investing in world-class research, driving innovation in public cancer services, addressing the unmet needs of cancer patients, and ensuring patients benefit from the best treatment, care services and supports. The Society is the strongest advocate in Irish life for the cancer patient and their families, and has been central to the development of government and public health policies in Ireland over decades.

The Irish Cancer Society directly provides supports and services to people affected by cancer, and is committed to expanding these, ensuring that such supports and services are easily accessible for all patients.

The Irish Cancer Society and AICRI share a goal to advance world-class cancer treatment and care, improve cancer outcomes, and improve quality of life for people affected by cancer across the island of Ireland.

About University College Dublin

University College Dublin (UCD) is a leading research-focused university in Europe, offering a range of academic programmes and engaging in research, innovation, and community involvement. It has played a pivotal role in shaping modern Ireland and is recognised globally, ranking among the top 1% of universities worldwide. With a diverse student body, Ireland's largest university, UCD contributes to national progress through interdisciplinary research and education.

At UCD, research and innovation are vital for economic growth, societal advancement, and cultural enrichment. With €161 million in grants secured in 2022/23 and over €1.3 billion since 2012/13, UCD drives collaborative projects addressing global challenges. The university leads in European Union research funding and has significantly increased its publication output, with papers cited above the global average. UCD fosters entrepreneurship through initiatives like NovaUCD, supporting over 550 companies and startups to commercialise research outputs for societal and economic impact.

About Queens University Belfast

Founded in 1845 as Queen's College Belfast, we became an independent university in 1908. Today, we are ranked 85 in the world for impact (Times Higher Education Impact Rankings 2023), and are a member of the Russell Group of UK research intensive universities, combining excellence in research and education with a student-centred ethos. Since 1845, our world-leading researchers, pioneering students and inspirational alumni have been making a difference to society in Belfast and across the world. Some of our renowned graduates include Nobel Prize winner Seamus Heaney, ‘Northern Ireland’s Einstein’ John Stewart Bell, and the father of emergency medicine James Francis Pantridge. Our cancer research is world renowned, an international reputation forged by Professor Patrick (Paddy) Johnston, and we continue to deliver on Paddy's legacy in a Centre that bears his name.
